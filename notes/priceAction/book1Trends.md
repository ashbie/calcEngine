### üìä **3. What will the whole chart look like after this reversal?**

## üßÆ PART 2: **Why Do Bond Prices Fall When Interest Rates Rise?**

It **does feel backwards at first**, but it makes sense once you understand how bonds work.

---

### üí° Let's take a simple example:

Let‚Äôs say you buy a **10-year Treasury bond** for **$1,000** that pays a **fixed 3% interest rate** per year (called the **coupon rate**).  
That means you earn **$30 per year** for the next 10 years.

---

### üö® Now imagine interest rates rise to **5%**

New bonds being issued now pay **$50/year** on the same $1,000 investment.

Your bond still only pays **$30/year**, which now looks **less attractive**.

üëâ So if someone wanted to sell that old 3% bond, they‚Äôd have to **sell it for less than $1,000** to make it competitive.

üîÅ That‚Äôs why when **interest rates go up**, **bond prices go down**.

---

### üìâ The Reverse is Also True:

If interest rates **fall** to, say, **2%**, then your 3% bond is suddenly a **great deal**, and people are willing to **pay more than $1,000** to get it.

So:
- üìà **Rates go down ‚Üí Prices go up**
- üìâ **Rates go up ‚Üí Prices go down**

---

### ‚öñÔ∏è Why This Matters for Treasury Futures:

When traders buy or sell Treasury futures, they‚Äôre **speculating** on future interest rate moves:

- If they **expect rates to fall**, they might **go long (buy)** the futures to profit from rising bond prices.
- If they **expect rates to rise**, they might **short (sell)** the futures, since bond prices will drop.
